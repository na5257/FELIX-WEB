# Science Narrative Options for FELIX

This document consolidates the main scientific gaps identified after reviewing the current website alongside the newly uploaded paper, `Role of neural oscillations in depression: highlights on gamma oscillations` (Yin & Li, 2026, article in press).

Its purpose is not to lock the website into one final framing. Instead, it gives a coherent view of the strongest scientific narratives now available, what they add, how they fit together, and how they could be translated into the site if you decide to update it.

## Executive Summary

The current science page already does two important things well:

- It frames depression as a problem of circuit timing and coordination, not just mood.
- It avoids the simplistic claim that depression always means "too little gamma."

The new paper strengthens that foundation and suggests a more mature scientific story:

- Gamma is not only a mechanism, but a possible biomarker.
- Depression-related gamma changes are heterogeneous across patients, tasks, and brain regions.
- Treatments as different as SSRIs, SNRIs, ketamine, rTMS, DBS, and sensory 40 Hz stimulation may converge partly through network retuning.
- Gamma depends on specific microcircuits, especially interactions involving PV+ and SST+ interneurons.
- Gamma is closely linked to synaptic plasticity, cross-frequency coupling, and network adaptability.
- Stress and inflammation are relevant parts of the gamma-depression story.
- The regional story is stronger when grounded in named circuits such as mPFC, hippocampus, nucleus accumbens, anterior cingulate, and temporal cortex.

Together, these additions make the FELIX rationale more persuasive, more current, and more clinically legible.

## The Main Narrative Upgrade

The strongest updated framing is this:

Depression may involve abnormal timing and coordination in mood-relevant brain circuits. Gamma oscillations offer one measurable window into that timing. FELIX therefore does not assume that 40 Hz stimulation is already a proven antidepressant treatment. Instead, it tests whether a tolerable sensory signal can influence gamma-related network function in ways that are measurable, biologically plausible, and clinically relevant.

This framing is stronger than a simple "40 Hz increases gamma and therefore helps depression" story because it:

- matches the scientific literature better
- accommodates heterogeneity between patients
- justifies EEG outcomes more clearly
- makes room for both mechanism and clinical uncertainty
- sounds more like translational neuroscience than marketing

## What the Current Website Underexplains

### 1. Why EEG and gamma matter clinically

The study page correctly lists EEG as a secondary endpoint, but the science page does not yet explain why this matters.

What is missing:

- a clear explanation that EEG provides an objective readout of circuit timing
- a link between gamma patterns and symptom severity, subtype, or treatment response
- an explanation that mechanistic measurements help test whether the intervention is acting on the intended brain process

Why this matters:

- users understand mood scales intuitively, but many do not understand why brain recordings are included
- adding this explanation strengthens the scientific credibility of the study design
- it clarifies that the trial is testing both outcome and mechanism

Recommended narrative:

Clinical questionnaires tell us whether participants feel and function differently. EEG may help show whether the intervention is also changing the timing and coordination of brain networks in a measurable way. In that sense, EEG is not just an add-on measurement, but part of testing the biological hypothesis behind FELIX.

### 2. Personalization and heterogeneity

The current site is already nuanced, but it does not yet fully exploit one of the review's strongest ideas: depression-related gamma changes are heterogeneous.

What is missing:

- baseline gamma state may influence response
- gamma can be high, low, poorly allocated, or weakly coordinated depending on region and task
- different patients may have different oscillatory signatures
- the same intervention may normalize gamma differently across subtypes and circuits

Why this matters:

- it prevents oversimplification
- it supports the logic of measuring EEG during the trial
- it positions FELIX as a mechanistic and potentially stratifiable intervention rather than a one-size-fits-all device

Recommended narrative:

The goal is not to drive every brain toward the same oscillatory profile. A more plausible goal is to help dysregulated networks move toward a more functional range of timing and coordination. This is one reason mechanistic readouts matter: they may help explain why some patients respond differently from others.

### 3. The monoamine-to-network bridge

The current site mentions antidepressants and ketamine, but it does not fully articulate the broader conceptual bridge from chemistry to circuits.

What is missing:

- a clear statement that many depression treatments may converge partly through retuning network dynamics
- a bridge between neurotransmitters and oscillations
- an explanation that gamma may sit downstream of multiple therapeutic pathways

Why this matters:

- it makes the FELIX story feel less isolated
- it positions 40 Hz stimulation within a larger and more credible treatment landscape
- it helps users understand why gamma is relevant even though depression has historically been framed in neurotransmitter terms

Recommended narrative:

Older models of depression emphasized neurotransmitter deficits alone. Newer work suggests that neurotransmitters, neuromodulation, and sensory interventions may all matter partly because they influence how circuits synchronize, communicate, and adapt over time. In that framework, gamma is not separate from serotonin, dopamine, ketamine, or rTMS, but one way of observing how these systems reshape network function.

### 4. Microcircuit specificity

The current site refers to inhibitory interneurons, but that can be sharpened.

What is missing:

- PV+ interneurons as fast-spiking timing organizers for gamma
- SST+ interneurons as additional contributors to gamma generation and regulation
- the idea that gamma reflects the integrity of specific inhibitory-excitatory microcircuits implicated in depression

Why this matters:

- it upgrades the biology from broad abstraction to circuit specificity
- it gives users a clearer sense of why gamma is biologically meaningful
- it aligns the site with the newer review literature

Recommended narrative:

Gamma oscillations emerge from tightly timed interactions between excitatory pyramidal neurons and inhibitory interneurons, especially parvalbumin-positive and somatostatin-positive cells. Because these same cell populations are implicated in stress-related and depressive pathology, gamma may provide a window into the health of the circuits treatment is trying to stabilize.

### 5. Synaptic plasticity

The current page hints at plasticity, but does not foreground it enough.

What is missing:

- gamma's link to LTP and plasticity induction
- cross-frequency coupling as a possible mechanism of network adaptation
- the idea that entrainment may be relevant not only to immediate synchronization, but also to learning and durable circuit change

Why this matters:

- it provides a stronger reason why repeated stimulation could matter
- it connects gamma to cognition, flexibility, and adaptation
- it helps the rationale move from "signal delivery" to "biological change"

Recommended narrative:

Gamma is not only a readout of moment-to-moment coordination. It is also linked to synaptic plasticity, including the conditions under which circuits strengthen, reorganize, and learn. This makes gamma a candidate mechanism through which repeated stimulation might influence cognition, affect regulation, and longer-lasting network behavior.

### 6. Stress and inflammation biology

The site currently discusses brain maintenance, glymphatic flow, and astrocyte-related themes, but the depression page itself could better integrate stress and inflammation.

What is missing:

- chronic stress models showing gamma disruption in depression-relevant circuits
- inflammation models where gamma deficits accompany depressive behaviors
- emerging evidence that 40 Hz stimulation may affect microglia and inflammatory signaling

Why this matters:

- it broadens the depression rationale beyond pure electrophysiology
- it links circuit dysfunction to known biological stress pathways
- it gives the 40 Hz story more depth without overclaiming human efficacy

Recommended narrative:

Gamma abnormalities in depression are not only discussed at the level of behavior or EEG. In preclinical models, chronic stress and inflammatory challenges can disrupt gamma activity in circuits relevant to mood and cognition. This suggests that oscillatory dysregulation may sit downstream of broader biological stress responses, and that entrainment-based approaches may intersect with inflammatory and plasticity-related processes.

### 7. Regional specificity

The current site is strong at the level of "network timing," but weaker at the level of named anatomy.

What is missing:

- medial prefrontal cortex as a central mood-regulation and executive-control node
- hippocampus for memory, contextual processing, and plasticity
- nucleus accumbens for motivation and reward-related circuitry
- anterior cingulate and temporal regions in clinical oscillation studies
- the idea that gamma findings vary by region rather than forming one global brain signature

Why this matters:

- it makes the science feel more grounded
- it helps explain why inconsistent gamma findings can still be meaningful
- it supports the heterogeneity narrative

Recommended narrative:

Depression-related gamma changes appear across several interconnected regions rather than in one single location. These include prefrontal, cingulate, temporal, hippocampal, and reward-related circuits. This regional specificity helps explain why some studies report reduced gamma, others increased gamma, and others altered coupling or connectivity: the abnormality may depend on which circuit is being measured, under what condition, and in which patient population.

## Coherent Master Narrative for the Website

If the website is to sound more scientifically mature after the new paper, the most coherent top-level narrative is:

1. Depression is not only about mood chemistry. It can also involve disruptions in how brain circuits coordinate in time.
2. Gamma oscillations are one important signature of that coordination.
3. Gamma abnormalities in depression are heterogeneous and circuit-specific, not one universal deficit.
4. Many treatments may influence recovery partly through retuning network dynamics, not only through chemical effects in isolation.
5. A tolerable 40 Hz sensory stimulus may provide an external timing signal capable of influencing gamma-related network function.
6. EEG matters because it helps test whether the intended circuit mechanism is changing, not only whether symptoms change.
7. FELIX therefore studies both clinical outcome and biological mechanism, while remaining careful not to claim established efficacy in advance.

## Strong Narrative Angles You Could Choose From

These are not mutually exclusive. They can be combined.

### Option A: From Chemistry to Circuits

Best if you want the site to feel modern, clinically literate, and conceptually broad.

Core message:

Depression has often been explained in chemical terms, but emerging neuroscience suggests that timing and coordination across brain networks are also important. FELIX belongs to this newer circuit-informed perspective.

Strengths:

- very intuitive
- creates a compelling bridge from old models to new models
- legitimizes EEG and gamma naturally

Risk:

- can sound too dismissive of traditional antidepressant models if phrased carelessly

### Option B: Mechanism First

Best if you want the site to emphasize scientific restraint and credibility.

Core message:

FELIX is not presented as already proven treatment. It is a mechanism-driven intervention designed to test whether non-invasive 40 Hz stimulation can influence measurable network physiology linked to depressive symptoms.

Strengths:

- highly credible
- aligns well with the actual study design
- helps avoid overclaiming

Risk:

- may sound less emotionally compelling if the language becomes too technical

### Option C: Biomarker and Personalization

Best if you want the site to sound future-oriented and translational.

Core message:

Gamma may eventually help identify patient subtypes, quantify treatment engagement, and explain why some people respond differently. FELIX contributes to that more precise model of care by measuring EEG alongside symptoms.

Strengths:

- clinically exciting
- strong justification for EEG
- makes the science page feel more forward-looking

Risk:

- must be carefully worded so it does not sound like biomarker-guided care is already established

### Option D: Circuit Repair and Plasticity

Best if you want to stress adaptation, recovery, and longer-term brain change.

Core message:

Gamma is not only about instantaneous synchronization. It is linked to the conditions under which brain circuits adapt, strengthen, and regain flexibility. This may help explain why repeated stimulation could matter.

Strengths:

- strong biological depth
- connects nicely to cognition and resilience
- supports repeated-use design logic

Risk:

- easiest place to accidentally overstate causal evidence in humans

## Recommended Combined Position

The best website update would likely combine:

- `From Chemistry to Circuits`
- `Mechanism First`
- `Biomarker and Personalization`

with a lighter touch of:

- `Circuit Repair and Plasticity`

That combination gives the most modern and coherent narrative while keeping the tone disciplined.

## Suggested Science Page Additions

Below are recommended additions by theme, not by exact page structure.

### Add a section or card on "Why EEG is included"

Purpose:

- explain the clinical and mechanistic role of EEG
- connect gamma to study design

Key message:

EEG may help reveal whether the intervention is engaging the intended brain mechanism, not only whether symptoms change.

Suggested wording:

"In FELIX, EEG is included because the study is testing more than symptom change alone. Gamma-related activity can provide an objective window into how brain circuits coordinate in time. By measuring EEG alongside clinical outcomes, the study can ask not only whether participants improve, but also whether improvement is accompanied by measurable changes in the targeted network dynamics."

### Add a paragraph on heterogeneity and personalization

Purpose:

- make the rationale more sophisticated
- justify why mechanism matters

Suggested wording:

"Current evidence suggests that gamma abnormalities in depression are heterogeneous. Depending on the circuit, task, and patient subgroup, gamma activity may appear reduced, excessive, poorly timed, or weakly coordinated across regions. This means the therapeutic goal is unlikely to be a uniform increase in gamma for every individual. A more plausible aim is to support movement toward a more functional pattern of network timing."

### Add a paragraph on convergent treatment logic

Purpose:

- situate FELIX within broader depression science

Suggested wording:

"This network perspective also helps connect seemingly different treatments. Monoaminergic antidepressants, ketamine, rTMS, DBS, and sensory entrainment may act through different entry points, yet all can influence how circuits synchronize and communicate. In that sense, gamma may serve as one shared window into treatment-relevant network retuning rather than the signature of a single intervention class."

### Sharpen the microcircuit biology

Purpose:

- make the mechanism more specific and credible

Suggested wording:

"At the cellular level, gamma depends on tightly timed interactions between excitatory pyramidal neurons and inhibitory interneurons, especially parvalbumin-positive and somatostatin-positive populations. These microcircuits help shape the narrow temporal windows needed for effective cortical communication, and growing evidence suggests they are themselves vulnerable in stress-related and depressive pathology."

### Add a plasticity paragraph

Purpose:

- explain why repeated entrainment might matter

Suggested wording:

"Gamma is also linked to synaptic plasticity: the processes through which neural circuits strengthen, reorganize, and adapt. Studies of cross-frequency coupling and plasticity induction suggest that oscillatory timing may help determine when networks are able to encode, update, or stabilize information. This makes gamma relevant not only to momentary coordination, but potentially to longer-lasting changes in cognition and affect regulation."

### Add a stress and inflammation paragraph

Purpose:

- deepen the depression model biologically

Suggested wording:

"Preclinical research also suggests that gamma dysregulation in depression can emerge in the context of chronic stress and inflammatory challenge. In animal models, stress-related and inflammation-related states can disrupt gamma activity in mood-relevant circuits, while emerging 40 Hz studies suggest possible effects on microglial and inflammatory pathways. These findings do not establish clinical efficacy, but they broaden the biological rationale for investigating gamma-targeted interventions."

### Add regional specificity language

Purpose:

- ground the science in real anatomy

Suggested wording:

"Gamma-related abnormalities in depression have been reported across multiple interconnected regions, including medial prefrontal, cingulate, temporal, hippocampal, and reward-related circuits such as the nucleus accumbens. This regional variation is important: it helps explain why the literature does not show one single directional change in every study, and why circuit context matters when interpreting gamma as a biomarker or treatment target."

## Suggested New Section Architecture

If you decide to restructure the science page, a cleaner and more complete architecture could be:

1. `Depression as a disorder of network timing`
2. `Why gamma matters`
3. `Why the findings are heterogeneous`
4. `How existing treatments may retune networks`
5. `What 40 Hz stimulation is trying to test`
6. `Why EEG is measured in FELIX`
7. `What remains uncertain`

This structure would let the site move from broad concept to mechanistic detail to study logic.

## What Should Be Stated Carefully

To keep the site scientifically strong, these boundaries should remain explicit:

- FELIX is testing a mechanistic and clinical hypothesis, not presenting established antidepressant efficacy.
- Gamma is promising as a biomarker candidate, but not yet a validated routine clinical biomarker for depression.
- The literature is heterogeneous, so normalization is a better framing than simple amplification.
- Preclinical findings on inflammation, microglia, plasticity, or broader brain maintenance should be presented as biological rationale, not patient-level proof.

## A Concise Website Positioning Statement

If you want one concise paragraph that captures the improved framing:

"FELIX is based on the idea that depression may involve not only symptoms of mood, but also altered timing and coordination across brain circuits involved in cognition, salience, and affect regulation. Gamma-band activity offers one measurable window into that coordination. Because gamma changes in depression appear to be heterogeneous across regions, tasks, and patient groups, the goal of 40 Hz stimulation is not simply to increase gamma indiscriminately, but to test whether a tolerable sensory signal can help move dysregulated networks toward a more functional pattern of timing. This is also why FELIX measures EEG: to examine whether clinical change is accompanied by measurable engagement of the proposed neural mechanism."
